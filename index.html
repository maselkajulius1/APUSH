Timeline Thesis

Gulf of Tonkin Resolution

In 1965, the United states invaded Vietnam in its quest to contain communism, using the justification of the new Gulf of Tonkin Resolution, based on a North Vietnamese attack in 1964 which was falsely construed by the NSA (National Security Agency). The United States vessel Maddox fired warning shots on three approaching North Vietnamese torpedo boats while performing an intelligence patrol and faced retaliation relatively unscathed, resulting in the death of 4 North Vietnamese individuals. The vessel reported that two days later, on August 4th, North Vietnamese boats attacked Maddox. Some doubts were cast on the event as soon as it happened, and it was later confirmed in 2003 by former secretary of defense Robert S. McNamara that no North Vietnamese vessels were present. The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution gave President Lyndon B. Johnson clearance to give military aid any country in South East Asia that was deemed jeopardized by the threat of communism.

1970

Nixon's Presidency

Nixon's presidency was muddled with much conversy. As president for most of the Vietnam War, he was bound to controversy, but furthered the polarity with his commentary and actions. Despite running on being hard on the war in Vietnam and claiming to end it decisively, the war ended 4 years after his election. Nixon was clear in his stance on the wartime protest, famously reffering to anti-war student protestors as "bums" following the sensitive Kent State Shooting. He later rescinded this comment saying it was too broadly applied to all opposed to the war, but added that he felt the word may be "too kind" for those participating in violence. Nixon also coined the term "Silent Majority", the group of conservative Americans who did not engage in anti-war sentiment and who Nixon claimed silently supported him and his Vietnam policies. Nixon's use of the term to radically seperate older conservative Americans from the growing activist youth had a lasting impact in the countries political divide. Most famously, Nixon's second term was tainted by the Watergate scandal. Nixon's re-election team was discovered bugging and burglurazing the Democratic Party headquaters in Washington. Despite never being proven as directly involved, Nixon attempted to cover up his administrations involvement, with Nixon partially expunging Oval Office recordings regarding the matter and the released "Smoking Gun Tapes" proving he ordered the CIA stop the FBI's ongiong investigate, leading to his impeachment and hastly resignation. Furthering the discontent, he was subsequently pardoned of his perjury charged by his Vice-President Gerald Ford

1969-1974

Reagan's Presidency

Reagans presidency was steeped in optimism in what he claimed he could do for the United States economy. Reagan was a hard promoter of "trickle-down economics", the idea that providing tax cuts to the most wealthy would promote business and eventually improve the economic standing of the working class. Despite increasing the nations gdp, Reagans domestic economic policy provided no relief to the general american, insignificantly changed unemployment and poverty rates. Reagans policies, also dubbed Reagonomics, remain in contention for needlessly raising the wealth desparity gap with some still in support of tax cuts believing that technological advancement and the economy require it as stimulation. This furthered the divide between conservative Republican Americans who saw this concept with optimism and Democrats seeing it as vastly damaging.

1981-1989

George W. Bush Presidency

President George W. Bush was another American president who had to address an attack on the countries citizens, this time on domestic grounds. 9/11 was a event that shocked the country like no other, and the political response was an increased effect of the idea of a stricter world government and foreign peacekeeping, now able to be justified as protecting that mainland. A month and a half after the events of 9/11 the USA PATRIOT (Uniting and Strengthening America by Providing Appropriate Tools Required to Intercept and Obstruct Terrorism) Act was written and passed. Clauses in the PATRIOT act enabled the United States government to perform new forms of surveillance on its citizens, including wiretimes, in the name of countering terrorism. The act also allowed internet service providers to expose users data to the government if deemed necessary in the events of a death or pertinent legal matter. A year following, the Department of Homeland Security was established, an executive department focused on domestic security through counterterrorism, immigration, and cybersecurity. The immediate response of the Bush administration in creating an act and organization that allowed to government to more tightly monitor the country sparked conspiracy that the attack was a deliberate excuse to monitor more heavily.

2001-2009

1965-1973

Vietnam War

The Vietnam war quickly grew highly controversial and faced heavy backlash from the nations youth. Dubbed the "Television War", it was the first time in history US citizens could see the atrocities of war from their homes with the advent of the common household television. In 1965, when US troops were deployed, the war immediately saw backlash from youth activists and college students. By 1968, increased deployment and threats of the draft had driven the controversy to become a mainstream issue. This was also the year the Tet Offensive occurred, a major escalation and surprise attack from the Viet Cong against South Vietnamese and United States troops, which convinced many Americans that this was not a winning war for the country. By 1972 US involvement was waning, and by 1973 the war officially ended with the Paris Peace Accords. The consequences of this war was a death total between 1.32 million and 3.43 million, including 58,220 Americans. Americans were acutely aware of the atrocities committed by both sides including mass civilian killings, rape, torture, and guerilla warfare.

1977-1981

Carter Presidency

Carters presidency was another tainted by controversy. Overall, his presidency was successful, focused on humanitarian, moralist, and conservationist policy and an honest outlook that was perhaps not enough optimistic for the American populace. At the end of his presidency Carter was faced with the Iranian hostage crisis. During an Iranian regime change 66 Americans at the US Embassy in Tehran were taken hostage. Leaders of the Iranian Revolution demanded prior Iranian leader Mohammed Reza Pahlavi, who had been granted asylum by Carters administration and given cancer treatment in the US, be relinquished to Iran. Hostage negotiations were refused until a president other than Carter was in place. This massively damaged his re-election and made many Americans feel as though the country could not protect its own citizens or put them first.

1977-1981

Clinton Presidency

Clinton was another president who largely degraded confidence in the executive branch. Like Nixon, the hallmark of his presidency was an absence of decorum and dishonesty before the public. While having largely uncontroversial accomplishments and policies, this was largely overshadowed by a long-term sexual affair with White House intern Monica Lewinsky. His affair was a heavily publicized scandal with him lying about his behavior in a court of law, netting him charges of perjury and using his seat as president to obstruct justice. He was the first president to be formerly impeached without resignation since Andrew Jackson, but was soon acquitted of his charges.
